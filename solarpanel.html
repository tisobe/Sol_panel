Solar Panel/Electric Power/Fine Sensor with Solar Angle

Summary

In this memo, we examine the evolution of solar panel temperatures, electric powers, and fine sensor temperatures with time. The data are subdivided into several solar angle intervals so that we can examine whether the solar angle affects the evolution of these quantities.

The fine sensor temperatures are already operating well above the temperatures that they did near launch and we must revise our operational thresholds upwards limit, and a few other quantities may exceed their early mission limits in the next few years. There seems no strong solar angle dependency among the quantities examined.

Solar Panel Temperature and Solar Angle

To understand whether the temperatures of the solar panels change as time passes, we plotted -Y SADA temperature, +Y SADA temperature, -Y solar panel temperature, and +Y solar panel temperature against mission time. The data are divided into a 20 degree intervals of the solar pitch angle starting at 40 degrees (minimum pitch is a little greater than 46) to search for any dependencies of these quantities on the solar angle, and then each data group is plotted against the time in years. The _blank spaces in the plotted data are due to missing data in MTA database.

Figure 1 shows the plots of the data of the angle between 80 and 100. The red lines indicate top and bottom envelopes, and an average of the data. For SADA data, all lines are estimated by a 4th degree polynomial fits to a 10 day moving box. The top envelope is the polynomial fits for the maximum values of each 10 day period, the bottom envelope is that of the minimum, and the middle line is that of the average.

For the solar arrays, a model of a linear fit plus sine function plus an exponential decay is used to estimate the average fit. The envelopes are set to 4 sigma away from the center line. The sine wave is induce by direct solar heating which is maximal in January when the Earth is closest to the Sun.

The green lines indicate the upper and lower limits of the MTA safe operation range.

Figure 1: Solar Panel Temperature Evolution (Solar Angle = 80 - 100 degree)

The lower envelopes of both + and - Y solar arrays are crossing the lower operation limits, but the envelopes may be inflated by the outliers (see the next paragraph). Other solar angle intervals, appear to stay in the operational ranges in foreseeable future.

Note that the solar panel temperature drops to 304K semi-regularly every 6 months or so (some cases 3 months, and in other cases, 1 year) for a very short time (up to 2 hrs). This occurs during the Earth eclipse seasons when the tempearature is expected to go down. One example is plotted on Figure 2. The green vertial lines indicate the timing of the eclipes.

The data with the solar angles 80 to 100 and 100 to 120 shows more of these temperature drops than the other periods, and this could be a cause of the inflated envelopes.

Spacecraft Electric Power and Solar Angle

Similar plots are made for spacecraft bus current/voltage (ELBI/ELBV), and electric power (HRMA power/OBA power). The currents is in Amp., voltage is Volts, and the power is in Watts. Figure 3 shows the plots of these quantities of the solar angle interval between 80 and 100. As before, the red lines are the envelopes and the averages obtained by the polynomial fits, except for ELBV, in which the average is computed by a linear/sine function combination. Again, the fitting was done only for data after year 2003 for ELBV.

The green lines indicate the operational ranges, but for HRMA and OBA power, the operational ranges are far beyond the plotting ranges, and do not show up in these plots.

Figure 3: Spacecraft Electric Power Evolution (Solar Angle = 80 - 100 degree)

ELBV in all solar angle ranges have downward trends, and it seems that eventually they will cross the lower operational limit.

Fine Sensor Temperature and Solar Angle

Fine sensor temperatures are the other quantities may behave differently with different solar angles. Figure 5 shows the plots for the fine sensor temperature of the solar angle interval between 80 and 100. As before, all red lines are computed by the polynomial fits method and the green lines are the operational ranges.

Figure 5: Fine Sensor Temperature Evolution (Solar Angle = 80 - 100 degree)

Although there are several temperature measures on the fine sensors, FFS brackets (TFSSBKT1/TFSSBKT2) and FSSE temperature (TPC_FSSE) are only measures which show a significant upward drift. No power related quantities (e.g., AFSSPC1V---FSS power converter voltage) show any signs of the drift.

The upper envelopes, and, in some cases, the averages of all three measures are crossing the upper operational limits as early as year 2003, and keep rising.
